Stars & Constellations

Patterns in the sky and the brilliant engines of the universe

What Are Stars?

Stars are massive spheres of burning plasma, primarily composed of hydrogen and helium. They generate light and heat through nuclear fusion, and serve as the fundamental building blocks of galaxies.

Types of Stars

  • Main Sequence Stars: Like our Sun, stable and steadily fusing hydrogen.
  • Red Giants: Expanding, aging stars nearing the end of their lives.
  • White Dwarfs: Dense remnants of stars that have exhausted their fuel.
  • Neutron Stars & Pulsars: Collapsed stars with extreme densities and magnetic fields.
  • Supernovae & Black Holes: End-of-life events for massive stars, creating violent explosions or gravitational sinkholes.

Constellations

Constellations are recognizable groupings of stars that form patterns in the night sky, often named after mythological figures or animals. They're used for navigation, storytelling, and sky mapping.

Notable examples include:

  • Orion: The hunter, visible across both hemispheres.
  • Ursa Major: Home to the Big Dipper asterism.
  • Scorpius: Resembling a scorpion, rich with bright stars.
  • Leo: The lion, prominent in spring skies.

Fun Facts

  • The closest star to Earth after the Sun is Proxima Centauri, 4.24 light-years away.
  • Some stars visible today may have already died; their light is still reaching us.
  • The color of a star reveals its temperature — blue is hottest, red is coolest.
  • There are more stars in the universe than grains of sand on Earth.
