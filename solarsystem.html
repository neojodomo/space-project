The Solar System

A dynamic celestial neighborhood around our Sun

Overview

The Solar System is our cosmic home—a gravitationally bound system of the Sun and all the objects that orbit it, including eight planets, over 200 moons, countless asteroids, comets, and dwarf planets like Pluto.

Structure of the Solar System

It is divided into the inner rocky planets (Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars), the outer gas and ice giants (Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune), the asteroid belt, and the Kuiper Belt beyond Neptune. At the very edge lies the Oort Cloud—home to long-period comets.

The Sun

At the heart of it all is the Sun, a massive G-type star that makes up 99.8% of the Solar System’s mass. Its gravity governs the motion of everything else in the system and provides the energy that sustains life on Earth.

Planetary Orbits

Planets orbit the Sun in elliptical paths on roughly the same plane—the ecliptic. Orbital periods vary: Mercury completes a year in 88 days, while Neptune takes 165 Earth years to circle the Sun.

Moons, Asteroids & Comets

Earth’s Moon is just one of many—Jupiter and Saturn have dozens. The asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter holds rocky remnants from the early solar system, while icy comets from the Kuiper Belt and Oort Cloud occasionally visit the inner solar system.

Why It Matters

The Solar System offers a glimpse into the formation of planetary systems across the galaxy. Studying its components helps us understand planetary evolution, habitability, and Earth’s place in the cosmos.
