Moons of the Solar System

Natural satellites that orbit planets and enrich our cosmic story

What Are Moons?

Moons, also known as natural satellites, are celestial bodies that orbit planets or dwarf planets. Our Solar System hosts over 200 known moons, ranging in size from small asteroid-like objects to massive bodies larger than planets like Mercury.

Earth’s Moon

Earth has one moon—the fifth largest in the Solar System. It influences tides, stabilizes Earth's tilt, and has been a central figure in mythology, science, and human exploration. In 1969, it became the first celestial body visited by humans.

Major Moons of the Solar System

Jupiter: Home to 95 moons including the Galilean moons—Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto. Ganymede is the largest moon in the Solar System.

Saturn: Known for Titan, a massive moon with a dense atmosphere and methane lakes. Saturn has over 145 confirmed moons.

Uranus: Its 27 moons are named after characters from Shakespeare and Alexander Pope's works, including Titania and Oberon.

Neptune: Triton, its largest moon, has retrograde motion and is geologically active.

Exploration and Scientific Interest

Moons like Europa, Titan, and Enceladus are prime candidates in the search for extraterrestrial life due to their subsurface oceans and complex chemistry. Missions like NASA’s upcoming Europa Clipper aim to investigate their potential habitability.

Fascinating Facts

  • Ganymede has its own magnetic field.
  • Enceladus has geysers that spew water vapor into space.
  • Titan has rivers, lakes, and possibly rainfall—all made of methane.
  • Io is the most volcanically active body in the Solar System.
