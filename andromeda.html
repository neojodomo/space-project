Andromeda Galaxy

The closest spiral galaxy to the Milky Way

Overview

The Andromeda Galaxy (M31) is a majestic spiral galaxy approximately 2.5 million light-years from Earth. As the nearest major galaxy to the Milky Way, it's visible to the naked eye from Earth under dark skies.

Structure & Composition

Andromeda has a structure similar to the Milky Way—comprising a central bulge, a galactic disk, and spiral arms filled with stars, gas, and dust. It’s roughly 220,000 light-years across and contains over a trillion stars—twice the number of stars in the Milky Way.

Future Collision

Andromeda and the Milky Way are on a collision course, expected to merge in about 4–5 billion years. This event will reshape both galaxies into a massive elliptical galaxy, possibly called "Milkomeda."

Observation & History

Recorded as early as 964 CE by Persian astronomer Al-Sufi, it was later catalogued by Charles Messier in 1764. Andromeda helped Edwin Hubble prove the existence of galaxies beyond the Milky Way in the 1920s, forever changing our view of the universe.
