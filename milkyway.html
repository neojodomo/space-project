Milky Way Galaxy

Our vast, spiral-shaped galactic home in the universe

Overview

The Milky Way is a barred spiral galaxy that hosts our solar system. It spans approximately 100,000 light-years in diameter and contains between 100 to 400 billion stars. From Earth, it's visible as a hazy band of light stretching across the night sky.

Structure

The galaxy features a central bulge surrounded by four major spiral arms, filled with stars, gas, and dust. It also has a galactic halo made up of globular clusters and dark matter. The entire galaxy orbits a supermassive black hole named Sagittarius A* at its center.

Location of Our Solar System

Our solar system resides in the Orion Arm, a minor spiral arm located between the larger Sagittarius and Perseus arms. We are about 27,000 light-years from the galactic center.

Motion and Rotation

The Milky Way rotates slowly, completing a single galactic year (one orbit around the center) in about 225 to 250 million Earth years. Stars, including our Sun, orbit within this massive gravitational structure.

Interesting Facts

  • It is on a collision course with the Andromeda galaxy, expected to merge in about 4 billion years.
  • The galaxy is surrounded by satellite galaxies such as the Magellanic Clouds.
  • Though vast, the Milky Way is only one of trillions of galaxies in the observable universe.
